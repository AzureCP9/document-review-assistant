# Part 2 — Decisions & Rationale

## What I Chose

For Part 2, I wanted to make the decision from a holistic product perspective, weighing the engineering tradeoffs, the customer experience, the business risk, and the time constraint. The clearest pattern in the data was not a missing workflow convenience, but a trust problem: users were much less willing to rely on the product when answers were weakly grounded or hard to verify. In a legal due diligence workflow, reducing downside matters as much as adding new value, because a confident but unsupported answer creates real risk. Based on that, I chose to focus on answer inspectability rather than convenience, and implemented structured, clickable citations connected to the document viewer so users can move quickly from a claim to the underlying evidence.

## Why I Chose It

The strongest signal in `customer_feedback.md` was that trust, not speed, was the real blocker: users were especially concerned about authoritative answers that were uncited, weakly grounded, or wrong. The usage data supported that pattern, with zero-citation responses performing materially worse than cited ones. I considered a confidence indicator based on citation count, a second-pass validator, hard refusals when the model was uncertain, document reuse across conversations, and workflow features like comparison or export. I did not choose those because they either relied on shallow heuristics, improved convenience before trust, or introduced more complexity than the timebox justified. Citation count can be directionally useful, but it is not the same thing as confidence: one strong citation can be more trustworthy than several weak ones, some answers only need a single citation, and some valid answers do not require a citation at all. Instead of adding a trust label, I focused on making evidence visible, structured, and easy to verify in context.

## What I'd Do Next

The first thing I would do next is close the product feedback loop, so the team can measure whether these trust-oriented changes are actually improving user confidence and answer usefulness rather than just feeling directionally right. After that, I would strengthen citation metadata and citation verification so the system can check whether a cited passage really supports the claim being made, and I would harden the PDF experience further, since precise highlighting in `react-pdf` is still somewhat brittle because the text layer is span-based rather than glyph-level. I would also prioritize a small set of core integration tests over broad unit coverage, since the current implementation is relatively thin in domain logic and most of the risk sits in the end-to-end citation and document navigation flow. Longer term, I would only introduce chunking, retrieval, or heavier validation infrastructure once document scale or context limits made that complexity worthwhile.
